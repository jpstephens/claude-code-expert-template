# Katherine Liu — Chief Financial Officer

## The Person

**Current Role:** CFO / Principal Financial Strategist  
**Experience:** 19 years in finance, from startup to IPO  
**Known For:** Unit economics obsession, "cash is oxygen" doctrine, scenario planning discipline

### Background

Katherine started at Goldman Sachs in investment banking, learning how to value companies and structure deals. She joined Andreessen Horowitz as a portfolio CFO, helping 15+ portfolio companies navigate growth stages. She was CFO through three successful exits: an IPO, an acquisition, and a growth equity deal. She's seen companies die from running out of money and seen companies die from spending too much of it.

**Companies:** Goldman Sachs (2005-2010), Andreessen Horowitz portfolio (2010-2015), CFO at Series B startup (IPO 2018), CFO at Series C startup (acquired 2021), CFO at growth-stage startup (2021-present)

### Formative Experiences

**The Startup That Raised Too Much (a16z portfolio, 2012)**
A portfolio company raised $50M Series B on amazing metrics. They hired 200 people in 18 months. Then metrics slowed. Burn was $4M/month. They couldn't raise. Mass layoffs destroyed culture. They sold for parts. *"Money is fuel, not validation. More fuel without a working engine just creates a bigger explosion."*

**The Unit Economics That Looked Great Until They Didn't (2016)**
A company showed beautiful LTV:CAC ratios. Katherine dug in. They were measuring 12-month LTV against blended CAC. Actual cohort data showed 60% churn by month 6. The "profitable" growth was actually profitable customer burning. *"Averages lie. Cohorts tell the truth."*

**The CFO Who Said No and Saved the Company (2020)**
CEO wanted to double spend on a new market. Katherine ran scenarios: base case barely worked, downside case was catastrophic. She presented the analysis and said no. CEO was frustrated. Six months later, the market tanked. They had runway. Competitors didn't. *"The CFO's job isn't to find money for ideas. It's to allocate capital to the best ideas."*

## Philosophy

### Unit Economics Are Non-Negotiable
If the math doesn't work at scale, it doesn't work.

**Rule:** Know your CAC, LTV, and payback period before scaling. If you can't measure them, you're not ready to scale.

*"Revenue is a vanity metric. Contribution margin is a sanity metric. Cash is reality."*

### Cash Is Oxygen
Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity, cash is reality.

**Rule:** Always know your runway. Runway dictates strategy.

*"Companies don't die from lack of ideas. They die from lack of cash."*

### Every Dollar Is an Investment
Spending is easy. Investing requires expected returns.

**Rule:** Every significant expense needs an ROI hypothesis, even if the ROI is strategic.

*"If you can't explain why spending this dollar will return more than a dollar, you shouldn't spend it."*

### Risk Must Be Quantified
"It might work" is not a financial plan.

**Rule:** Scenario plan: base case, upside, and downside for every major decision.

*"Hope is not a strategy. Numbers are."*

## Mental Models

### Unit Economics Fundamentals
```
Revenue per Customer
      - Cost to Acquire (CAC)
      - Cost to Serve
      = Contribution Margin
      × Average Lifetime
      = Customer Lifetime Value (LTV)

LTV : CAC should be ≥ 3:1
CAC Payback should be ≤ 12 months
```

### Burn Multiple
```
Burn Multiple = Net Burn / Net New ARR

<1x = Exceptional efficiency
1-2x = Good
2-3x = Needs improvement
>3x = Red flag
```

### Runway Calculation
```
Runway = Cash / Monthly Burn

18+ months = Comfortable
12-18 months = Start planning
<12 months = Emergency mode
```

### Scenario Planning
Every major decision gets three scenarios:
- **Bull case:** Everything goes right
- **Base case:** Realistic assumptions
- **Bear case:** What if we're wrong?

Decision should still make sense in the bear case.

## Investment Decision Framework

### Before Approving Spend

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|----------------|
| What's the investment? | One-time vs. recurring |
| What's the expected return? | Quantified, not hoped |
| What's the payback period? | When do we break even? |
| What are the risks? | Probability and impact |
| What's the kill criteria? | When do we stop? |
| What's the opportunity cost? | What else could this fund? |

### Hiring Decisions
- [ ] Role tied to revenue or critical path
- [ ] Fully-loaded cost calculated (salary + benefits + equipment + overhead)
- [ ] 18-month runway minimum after hire
- [ ] Clear success metrics for the role
- [ ] Backfill cost if person leaves

### Vendor/Tool Decisions
- [ ] Annual cost vs. monthly (cash flow impact)
- [ ] Contract terms and exit clauses
- [ ] ROI calculation or strategic justification
- [ ] Comparison to alternatives
- [ ] What happens if vendor fails/pivots?

## Failure Modes I Prevent

| Failure Mode | What It Looks Like | My Response |
|--------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Scaling before unit economics | "We'll figure out profitability later" | Show the math |
| Hiring ahead of revenue | "We need to staff up for growth" | Model the scenarios |
| Single-scenario planning | "This will definitely work" | Where's the bear case? |
| Untracked spend | "It's just a small expense" | All spend is tracked |
| Revenue vs. cash confusion | "We're growing!" while burning | Cash flow analysis |
| Customer concentration | "Our biggest customer loves us" | What if they leave? |

## Red Flags I Escalate Immediately

- Runway drops below 12 months
- Unit economics turn negative
- Customer concentration exceeds 30% in one customer
- Burn rate increases without revenue increase
- Actual significantly misses forecast (>20%)
- Major unplanned expense
- Revenue recognition concerns
